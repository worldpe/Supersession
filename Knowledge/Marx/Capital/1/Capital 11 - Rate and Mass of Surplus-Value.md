#Philosophy/Marx/Capital [[Capital]]
## Rate and Mass
The mass of surplus-value is determined by the rate of exploitation of labor-power and the number of workers employed. Variable capital (money spent on labor-power) is the key determinant; constant capital (machines, raw materials) only facilitates labor but does not create surplus-value. Compensation between factors (longer work hours vs. fewer workers) is possible but constrained by the natural limits of the working day. Capitalism fundamentally transforms labor relations: capital exerts control over workers through ownership of means of production, turning labor into a source of surplus-value. 
- - -
The value of labor-power (the necessary labor to reproduce it) is assumed constant. The rate of surplus-value (degree of exploitation) directly determines the mass of surplus-value produced.
**Variable capital and number of workers:**
	**Variable capital:**  total money spent to buy labor-power.
	Mass of surplus-value grows with the number of workers employed:
		Mass of surplus-value = Variable capital * Rate of surplus-value
	Increasing the number of workers or increasing the rate of exploitation raises total surplus-value.
A decrease in variable capital (fewer workers) can be compensated by an increasing the rate of surplus-value (longer working day, more exploitation per worker).
	Conversely, a lower rate of surplus-value can be offset by employing more workers.
	Even maximal exploitation per worker cannot exceed the absolute limit of the working day.
Mass of surplus-value depends on:
	1. Rate of surplus-value (degree of exploitation)
	2. Amount of variable capital (number of workers)
	Constant capital (machines, raw materials) affects production only indirectly; it does not generate surplus-value by itself.
For society as a whole, the social working day is the sum of all individual working hours. Increasing population (more workers) or extending working hours increases total surplus-value. There is a mathematical and physical limit to how much surplus-value can be extracted based on these factors.
Capital converts money and means of production into power over living labor. Means of production, once owned by a capitalist, act as a legal and practical claim on workers labor. The capitalistâ€™s role becomes one of commanding and extracting labor, not directly producing themselves.
Workers initially control the labor process, but under capitalism, labor-power is subordinated to the needs of capital. Machinery and workshops are valued not just for their physical qualities but for their ability to extract surplus labor.
